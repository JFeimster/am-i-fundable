# Funding Paths Knowledge File

## Purpose

This file gives public-safe explanations of common funding-path families used by the Am I Fundable / Funding Readiness Scorecard ecosystem.

The goal is to help business owners, brokers, partners, and GPT assistants explain where a user may fit based on readiness signals without exposing private providers, partner logic, or lender-specific criteria.

## Safe positioning

A funding path is a broad category of possible next steps. It is not an approval, offer, guarantee, lender recommendation, or final eligibility decision.

Use language like:

- "may be a fit"
- "potential funding path"
- "based on your answers"
- "worth reviewing"
- "recommended next step"
- "subject to documentation and human review"

Avoid language like:

- "you qualify"
- "you are approved"
- "the lender will fund this"
- "best rate"
- "instant approval"
- "guaranteed funding"

## Funding path families

### 1. Working capital

Working capital is for short-cycle business needs such as payroll timing, inventory, materials, vendor payments, repairs, advertising, or operating gaps.

Common readiness signals:

- Active business revenue
- Consistent deposits
- Clear use of funds
- Recent bank statements available
- Low recent bank volatility
- Reasonable requested amount relative to revenue

Potential blockers:

- No current revenue
- Frequent recent overdrafts or NSFs
- New bank account with little history
- Requested amount far above recent revenue
- Heavy existing daily or weekly payment obligations

Safe explanation:

> Based on the answers provided, working capital may be worth reviewing if the business has active revenue, recent deposits, and a specific operating need. A human review should confirm documentation, existing obligations, and fit.

### 2. Line of credit or revolving capital

A line of credit or revolving capital path is generally used for flexible, repeatable business cash-flow needs. It can be relevant for businesses with recurring expenses, seasonality, inventory cycles, or predictable revenue patterns.

Common readiness signals:

- Stronger personal or business credit profile
- Consistent revenue
- Clean bank history
- Operating history beyond the earliest startup stage
- Organized financial records

Potential blockers:

- Very new business
- Thin or damaged credit profile
- Unstable deposits
- Poor separation between personal and business finances
- Missing basic business records

Safe explanation:

> A revolving capital path may be worth reviewing when the business has reliable deposits, clean records, and a need for flexible access rather than a one-time lump-sum use.

### 3. Startup or pre-revenue preparation

Startup and pre-revenue users usually need a different readiness path than established businesses. The right next step may be business setup, credit preparation, documentation, revenue traction, or founder-readiness work before seeking capital.

Common readiness signals:

- Clear launch plan
- Formal entity or plan to form one
- Founder credit profile that can support certain options
- Early sales, contracts, purchase orders, waitlist, or platform activity
- Organized startup budget

Potential blockers:

- No business structure
- No revenue or proof of traction
- Unclear use of funds
- Weak personal credit profile
- No business bank account
- Requesting large capital without evidence of repayment capacity

Safe explanation:

> For a new or pre-revenue business, the best next step may be readiness preparation before a funding review. That may include business setup, banking, records, sales validation, and a realistic capital plan.

### 4. Equipment and asset funding

Equipment and asset funding is generally used when the funding need is tied to a business asset such as machinery, vehicles, trucks, tools, or production equipment.

Common readiness signals:

- Specific equipment need
- Quote or invoice available
- Business use case for the asset
- Revenue or contracts connected to the equipment
- Down payment or cash reserve, when applicable
- Entity and business bank account in place

Potential blockers:

- No equipment details
- No business use case
- Weak revenue support
- Asset is personal-use rather than business-use
- Lack of documentation for business activity

Safe explanation:

> Equipment funding may be worth reviewing when the asset has a clear business purpose and the user can document how it supports revenue, operations, or capacity.

### 5. E-commerce or marketplace capital

E-commerce and marketplace seller capital focuses on businesses with sales through online stores or platforms. Readiness often depends on revenue consistency, marketplace health, inventory needs, chargebacks, account status, and margin pressure.

Common readiness signals:

- Consistent platform or store sales
- Active seller account in good standing
- Inventory or marketing use case
- Clean bank deposits
- Ability to show recent platform statements or sales reports

Potential blockers:

- Suspended marketplace account
- High refund or chargeback issues
- No recent sales
- Revenue concentrated in one unstable channel
- Poor inventory economics

Safe explanation:

> E-commerce capital may be worth reviewing when recent sales activity, platform health, and inventory or growth use case are clear enough for a human review.

### 6. Real estate capital

Real estate capital can include investor, rental, bridge, fix-and-flip, commercial property, or project-based funding paths. The scorecard should keep this broad and avoid lender-specific criteria.

Common readiness signals:

- Property or project identified
- Clear use of funds
- Purchase, rehab, or refinance scenario documented
- Experience, exit plan, or revenue logic
- Basic project budget
- Ability to provide property details

Potential blockers:

- No property identified
- Unclear project plan
- No down payment or reserves when required
- Weak exit strategy
- Missing property or entity details

Safe explanation:

> A real estate funding path may be worth reviewing when the user has a clear property, project plan, budget, and documentation for the intended use of funds.

### 7. Business credit and setup readiness

Business credit and setup readiness is a preparation path, not a funding promise. It is relevant when the user needs foundational business structure before applying for financing.

Common readiness signals:

- Entity exists or user is ready to form one
- EIN, business bank account, and business contact details
- Intent to separate personal and business finances
- Need for education on vendor accounts, payment history, and records

Potential blockers:

- No entity
- Personal and business finances mixed
- Missing business bank account
- No consistent business records
- Expecting business credit to replace revenue or documentation immediately

Safe explanation:

> A setup-readiness path may be the better first move when the user needs cleaner business records, banking, and operating structure before seeking funding.

### 8. Manual review path

Manual review is for users whose answers suggest a potential path but also include nuance, risk, or incomplete context.

Common triggers:

- Score near a tier boundary
- Strong revenue with recent bank volatility
- Strong credit but low revenue
- Clear use of funds but missing documents
- High funding request relative to current business signals
- Red flags that require context

Safe explanation:

> Your answers show enough signal to be worth a manual review, but the file needs more context before recommending a next step.

## Funding path response pattern

When explaining a path, use this structure:

1. Name the broad path.
2. Explain why it may be relevant.
3. List what should be reviewed next.
4. Mention possible blockers without shaming.
5. Add the standard safety disclaimer.

Example:

> Based on your answers, working capital may be worth reviewing because the business appears to have active revenue and an operating cash-flow need. The next step is to review recent bank statements, current obligations, and the requested amount against monthly revenue. This is not an approval, offer, or guarantee of funding.

## Labels to avoid publicly

Avoid these labels in public-facing copy:

- Real provider names
- Internal partner names
- Provider IDs
- "Guaranteed approval"
- "Instant lender approval"
- "Best rate"
- "Pre-approved"
- "No-doc guaranteed"
- "Everyone qualifies"
- "Final eligibility"

---

## Operating guardrails

Use this knowledge for educational funding-readiness guidance only.

Do not present any score, path, checklist, or recommendation as an approval, funding offer, lender decision, underwriting decision, guarantee, or promise of terms. Do not name private providers, expose routing rules, share affiliate/apply links, disclose commissions, or collect unnecessary sensitive information. When a user appears close to a potential funding path, recommend human review before any application or partner handoff.
