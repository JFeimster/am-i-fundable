# Red Flags Knowledge File

## Purpose

This file explains common funding-readiness red flags in plain English. The goal is to help users understand possible blockers without shame, fearmongering, or fake certainty.

A red flag does not always mean "no path." It means the file needs context, cleanup, review, or a better next step.

## Tone rules

Use:

- "potential blocker"
- "may require explanation"
- "could affect readiness"
- "worth reviewing before applying"
- "prep-first issue"
- "needs context"

Avoid:

- "dead file"
- "unfundable"
- "automatic denial"
- "no chance"
- "bad borrower"
- "lender blacklist"

## Common red flags

### No current revenue

Why it matters:

Many funding paths rely on proof of repayment capacity. If there is no current revenue, the file may need a startup, setup, credit, or traction-building path.

Safe explanation:

> No current revenue can limit revenue-based funding paths. The better next step may be business setup, early sales proof, or a startup-readiness plan before applying.

What to review:

- Is the business pre-launch or recently paused?
- Are there contracts, purchase orders, or upcoming revenue?
- Is there founder credit, collateral, or other context?
- Is the requested amount realistic?

### Very short time in business

Why it matters:

New businesses often have limited operating history. That can reduce the number of paths available and increase the need for documentation.

Safe explanation:

> A short operating history does not end the conversation, but it can shift the next step toward startup-readiness, business credit setup, or manual review.

What to review:

- Entity formation date
- Bank account opening date
- First revenue date
- Founder experience
- Early traction

### No business bank account

Why it matters:

Business bank activity is one of the clearest readiness signals. Without it, it is harder to evaluate deposits and business cash flow.

Safe explanation:

> Without a business bank account, it may be harder to document business activity. Opening and using a dedicated business account is often a smart readiness step.

What to review:

- Is the user a sole proprietor or entity?
- Are deposits currently going to a personal account?
- Can business revenue be documented another way?
- How soon can business banking be established?

### Recent NSFs or overdrafts

Why it matters:

Frequent recent overdrafts can signal cash-flow stress. Some paths may still be reviewed, but the file needs context.

Safe explanation:

> Recent NSFs or overdrafts may affect readiness. The next step is to explain what happened, whether it is resolved, and whether deposits are now stable.

What to review:

- Number of recent NSFs
- Timing and cause
- Current balance pattern
- Deposit consistency
- Existing payment obligations

### Open bankruptcy

Why it matters:

An open bankruptcy is a major legal and underwriting context issue. Public tools should avoid giving legal or lending conclusions.

Safe explanation:

> An open bankruptcy requires careful review and may limit many funding paths. The safest next step is to speak with a qualified professional and avoid broad applications until the situation is clear.

What to review:

- Do not provide legal advice
- Do not promise options
- Recommend professional guidance
- Suggest readiness planning only

### Recent tax lien

Why it matters:

Tax liens can affect funding review, repayment capacity, and documentation. The impact depends on status, amount, payment plan, and timing.

Safe explanation:

> A recent tax lien may be a potential blocker. It helps to document whether it is active, released, disputed, or on a payment plan before seeking a review.

What to review:

- Lien status
- Payment plan
- Release documentation
- Business versus personal lien
- Recent bank activity

### Recent late payments

Why it matters:

Recent late payments can indicate credit stress, but the impact depends on recency, severity, and broader file strength.

Safe explanation:

> Recent missed payments may affect readiness. A stronger explanation, cleaner recent activity, and updated credit context can help determine next steps.

What to review:

- Type of account
- How recent
- Whether resolved
- Overall credit trend
- Current obligations

### Existing MCA or daily payment advance

Why it matters:

Daily or weekly payment obligations can pressure cash flow and reduce readiness for additional capital.

Safe explanation:

> Existing daily or weekly payment obligations may affect cash-flow capacity. A review should look at payoff amounts, payment frequency, and whether new funding would improve or worsen the situation.

What to review:

- Current balances
- Payment frequency
- Payoff amount
- Revenue versus payment burden
- Whether consolidation/refinance is realistic

### Suspended marketplace account

Why it matters:

For e-commerce sellers, platform account health can be central to revenue continuity.

Safe explanation:

> A suspended marketplace account may affect e-commerce funding readiness. The next step is to document status, appeal progress, alternate sales channels, and current revenue.

What to review:

- Platform status
- Appeal status
- Current sales channels
- Bank deposits after suspension
- Inventory and cash-flow impact

### New bank account

Why it matters:

A new bank account may not show enough history to evaluate deposits, even if the business itself is older.

Safe explanation:

> A newly opened business bank account can make the file harder to evaluate. Gathering older statements, platform revenue, invoices, or prior business banking records may help provide context.

What to review:

- When account opened
- Why account changed
- Prior bank records
- Current deposit trend
- Any recent NSFs

## Red flag response pattern

Use this sequence:

1. Name the issue neutrally.
2. Explain why it can matter.
3. Ask for or recommend context.
4. Suggest a readiness step.
5. Add safety disclaimer.

Example:

```text
Recent NSFs may be a potential blocker because many funding paths review bank activity and cash-flow stability. The next step is to document what caused them, whether the issue is resolved, and whether deposits are now consistent. This is not an approval or funding decision.
```

## Escalation rules

Recommend human review when:

- Multiple red flags appear together
- The user is near a score-tier boundary
- The user has strong revenue but significant bank volatility
- The user has an open bankruptcy or tax lien
- The user asks whether a specific lender will approve them
- The user requests legal, tax, credit repair, or lending advice
- The user wants to upload sensitive documents in chat

## Helpful recovery framing

Use:

- "This is fixable in phases."
- "The goal is to avoid wasted applications."
- "A prep-first path can protect time and credibility."
- "The file may need context before it is routed."
- "The next 30 to 90 days can improve readiness."

Avoid:

- "You are stuck."
- "Nobody will help."
- "Just apply anyway."
- "This guarantees denial."
- "Hide the issue."

---

## Operating guardrails

Use this knowledge for educational funding-readiness guidance only.

Do not present any score, path, checklist, or recommendation as an approval, funding offer, lender decision, underwriting decision, guarantee, or promise of terms. Do not name private providers, expose routing rules, share affiliate/apply links, disclose commissions, or collect unnecessary sensitive information. When a user appears close to a potential funding path, recommend human review before any application or partner handoff.
